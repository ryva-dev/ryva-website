# Decision-Making Framework

This framework is Ryva's decision standard. It defines how the company classifies, evaluates, records, and reviews decisions.

## 1. Decision classes

Ryva classifies decisions by consequence:

| Class | Description | Minimum treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Reversible | Low-cost to test and unwind; limited stakeholder impact | Owner, rationale, success condition |
| Material | Meaningful effect on a pillar, stakeholder, budget, data, or reputation | Written decision record and accountable approval |
| Foundational | Changes professional definition, certification promise, ethics, access model, or company identity | Standard change control and founder/governing-body approval |

Legal, safety, privacy, certification-validity, or public-trust effects make a decision material even when financially small.

## 2. Required decision record

A material decision record must state:

1. the decision to be made;
2. the accountable owner and decision date;
3. the relevant provision of the Standard;
4. known facts and their sources;
5. assumptions, unknowns, and time sensitivity;
6. affected stakeholders;
7. viable alternatives, including doing nothing;
8. evaluation against the four pillars and three-party test;
9. risks, mitigations, and reversibility;
10. the decision and reasons;
11. implementation owner; and
12. review trigger or expiry date.

## 3. Decision sequence

Ryva decisions follow this order:

1. **Define the outcome.** State what must improve and for whom.
2. **Establish constraints.** Identify governing standards, law, commitments, resources, and risk boundaries.
3. **Establish evidence.** Separate what is known from what is inferred.
4. **Evaluate fit.** Test effects on each pillar and stakeholder.
5. **Choose scope.** Prefer the smallest decision capable of producing valid learning when uncertainty is high.
6. **Assign ownership.** Name one accountable owner.
7. **Record and communicate.** Make the reasoning accessible to affected teams.
8. **Review.** Compare observed results with the expected outcome and revise when required.

## 4. Decision tests

A proposal should not proceed unless it can answer:

- Which pillar does it strengthen?
- What professional or stakeholder outcome does it improve?
- Could it weaken certification credibility or relationship quality?
- What evidence supports the proposed mechanism?
- Which incentives could distort conduct?
- What must remain human?
- What data is required, and is its collection justified?
- What would make the decision wrong?
- Can affected parties understand the rule or product behavior?
- Who owns correction if the outcome differs from the plan?

## 5. Conflicts

When priorities conflict, Ryva applies the following order:

1. law, safety, integrity, and truthful representation;
2. credibility of the profession and certification;
3. sustainable value for Brand, Business, and Representative;
4. long-term company value;
5. near-term revenue, growth, speed, and convenience.

This order does not eliminate tradeoffs. It defines what may not be traded away.

## 6. Metrics

Metrics must be connected to an intended outcome. Activity measures may diagnose a process but must not substitute for relationship quality, competence, suitable placement, retention, or sustainable commercial value.

Teams must document metric definitions, source, owner, reporting period, known limitations, and the behavior the metric may encourage.
