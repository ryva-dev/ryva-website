# RF-07: The Ryva Retail Readiness Standard

## Purpose

The Retail Readiness Standard determines whether a Product and Brand can responsibly support an offer to a physical retailer or other Business Buyer. It answers an operational question, not a popularity question.

**Popular online does not mean retail ready.**

## Use

Use before Buyer presentation, after a material Product, packaging, pricing, logistics, claims, or policy change, and when entering a new category, channel, territory, or Business type. “Retail” is the name of the framework; category and buyer-type variants may apply to non-retail Businesses.

Do not use it as Product-quality proof, legal approval, or Business Fit.

## Inputs

- Product identity, specifications, category, packaging, labels, and current samples;
- wholesale and suggested retail pricing, MOQ, case packs, and identifiers;
- claims, substantiation, insurance considerations, and category requirements;
- inventory, lead times, shipping, returns, payment, and ordering processes;
- sales materials, samples, support, and reorder process;
- intended Business type, territory, and proposed terms; and
- evidence status, owners, effective dates, and unresolved conditions.

## Readiness classes

### Required readiness

Elements necessary to make an accurate and operable offer in the defined context:

- current wholesale pricing and currency;
- suggested retail pricing or an explicit statement that none is provided;
- Business margin analysis under current terms;
- minimum orders and case packs;
- Product identity, specifications, and ordering units;
- required barcodes or identifiers where applicable;
- compliant and accurate packaging and labels for the intended context;
- supportable Product claims and approved materials;
- sufficient inventory or a truthful availability plan;
- lead times, shipping terms, and delivery responsibilities;
- returns, damage, defect, and cancellation process;
- ordering process and authorized contacts;
- payment terms and invoicing process;
- support responsibilities; and
- reorder process.

An item may be “not applicable” only with a documented context-specific reason.

### Preferred readiness

Elements that improve adoption, efficiency, or trust but may not be essential in every context:

- samples or testers;
- high-quality Product and merchandising assets;
- staff education;
- display guidance;
- electronic ordering and status visibility;
- documented service levels;
- historical sell-through or use evidence;
- promotional calendar and approved co-marketing support;
- issue escalation and recovery targets; and
- structured feedback and reorder review.

### Category-specific readiness

Category requirements may include shelf life, lot or batch tracking, temperature control, ingredients, allergens, warnings, safety data, certifications, restricted claims, age controls, insurance, installation, maintenance, sanitation, training, or disposal.

Ryva must not invent a universal checklist for regulated or specialist categories.

### Unknown or unresolved readiness

Unknown means evidence is missing or not verified. Unresolved means a known discrepancy, condition, or approval remains open. Neither is equivalent to ready.

## Readiness register

For each element, record:

- **Required**, **Preferred**, **Category-specific**, or **Not applicable**;
- **Ready**, **Conditional**, **Not ready**, or **Unknown**;
- evidence source and date;
- owner;
- condition or missing item;
- due or review date; and
- Buyer-facing implication.

## Key element guidance

| Element | Minimum evidence | Common failure |
|---|---|---|
| Wholesale and suggested retail pricing | Current authorized price sheet and effective date | Calculating margin from stale public prices |
| Retailer margin | Transparent formula under proposed terms | Omitting freight, discounts, or required costs |
| MOQ and case packs | Current ordering terms by Product | Treating negotiable as agreed |
| Barcodes | Actual identifiers and applicability decision | Assuming every Business requires the same standard |
| Packaging and labels | Current samples/specifications and category review | Using mockups as finished readiness |
| Claims | Approved claim list and substantiation route | Reusing social copy without review |
| Insurance | Applicable requirement and current evidence where required | Representing insurance adequacy without qualified review |
| Samples | Availability, cost, handling, and approved use | Promising samples the Brand cannot supply |
| Inventory | Dated availability and allocation method | Treating a snapshot as reliable capacity |
| Lead times and shipping | Written process, terms, and service evidence | Quoting best-case time as commitment |
| Returns | Written policy, responsibilities, reason handling | Concealing cost or ambiguity |
| Sales materials | Current, accurate, authorized assets | AI-generated claims or obsolete line sheets |
| Ordering and payment | Approved workflow, contacts, terms, and security | Collecting sensitive payment data improperly |
| Support and reorder | Owners, routes, timing, and escalation | Assuming the Representative owns every issue |

## Decision method

1. Define Product, Brand, category, Business type, territory, and proposed terms.
2. Identify required and category-specific elements.
3. Gather and classify evidence.
4. Resolve any automatic stop condition, including credible unlawful claim, material safety risk, or inability to fulfill.
5. Record every readiness element.
6. Assign the most conservative supported outcome:
   - **Not Ready:** any material required element is Not ready, a stop condition exists, or missing evidence prevents a responsible offer.
   - **Conditionally Ready:** required elements can support a limited defined offer, but documented conditions, limits, or verification remain.
   - **Retail Ready:** all applicable required elements are Ready, category requirements are addressed, and remaining preferred gaps do not make the offer misleading or inoperable.
7. State scope, limits, next action, owner, and expiry.

## Output and action

The output is **Not Ready**, **Conditionally Ready**, or **Retail Ready**, plus scope, evidence confidence, missing elements, conditions, risks, and next action.

Conditionally Ready must specify what may be offered, to whom, under which limits, and what cannot yet be claimed.

## Guardrails

- Retail Ready does not mean commercially attractive or suitable for every Business.
- Online popularity, consumer packaging, and direct-to-consumer fulfillment do not prove wholesale readiness.
- Category-specific legal and safety questions require qualified review.
- A Representative cannot waive a required readiness condition to preserve commission.
- AI may track documents and gaps; a person approves readiness and Buyer-facing claims.

## Implementation Across Ryva

### Certification

Teach pricing, margin, MOQ, case packs, identifiers, packaging, claims, insurance considerations, inventory, logistics, returns, ordering, payment, support, and reorders. Assess a readiness audit and require a scoped outcome in the final project.

### Ryva Pro

Create category-aware checklists, evidence attachments, effective dates, owners, dependencies, status, expiry, and alerts. Block Present when required readiness is unresolved unless an authorized conditional path is recorded. Preserve audit history.

### Closing Program

Use readiness to prepare accurate answers, terms, samples, order design, and operational objections. Do not teach Representatives to “close through” a readiness failure.

### Marketing

Ryva may describe Retail Readiness as structured operational diligence. It must not imply regulatory certification, Product safety approval, or guaranteed acceptance by retailers.
